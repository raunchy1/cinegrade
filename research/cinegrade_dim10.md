## 6. The "Teal and Orange" Look: Origins, Overuse, Modern Variations

### Claim
Teal and Orange was invented by Stefan Sonnenfeld at Company 3 for Michael Bay's "Bad Boys II" (2003), based on complementary color theory applied to skin tones. It became a "virus" in Hollywood due to digital intermediate (DI) technology enabling precise color manipulation. Modern variations are more subtle, and the look has shifted toward social media creators who use it as a "starter cinematic look."

## 7. Bleach Bypass Aesthetic: Origins in Film, Digital Recreation

### Claim
Bleach bypass originated as a photochemical process where the bleach step was skipped in color negative processing, leaving metallic silver in the emulsion alongside color dyes. It creates desaturated colors, increased contrast, deeper blacks, and a pastel-like quality. Digital recreation uses LUTs, contrast curves, and desaturation to mimic the effect.

### Source
Cinematography.net / Roger Deakins Forum / AAA Presets

### URLs
- https://www.cinematography.net/edited-pages/BLEACH.htm [^472^]
- https://aaapresets.com/blogs/davinci-resolve-color-grading-gradient-tutorials/unlocking-cinematic-magic-teal-orange-bleach-bypass-and-cross-processing-explained [^478^]
- https://www.rogerdeakins.com/forums/topic/enr-process-with-digital/ [^484^]

### Date
2005-2023 (ongoing discussion)

### Excerpts
> "Basically, the color negative process uses a first developer to produce B&W images on all three color layers... If you reduce or eliminate the bleach, you have a metallic silver B&W image superimposed on your color image. This gives you a nice pastel effect and it also gives you as much as a full stop more film speed." [^472^]

> "No BLEACH leaves all the silver image in the film as well as the dye image. The end result is a denser image with all the colors and 'bulletproof' blacks. The image is therefore very desaturated and more contrasty." [^472^]

> "He shot 'Seven' in Super35, mostly on 5293 pushed one stop; he also flashed the negative. Then the print was bleach-bypassed... the pushing increased saturation, while the flashing lowered it and the contrast, and the bleach-bypass added contrast and lowered saturation." [^472^]

### Context
Bleach bypass represents a "prestige" film look associated with David Fincher's "Seven" (1995), "Saving Private Ryan," and "Fight Club." The digital preset market offers "bleach bypass" LUTs that simulate this without understanding the photochemical complexity. For CINEGRADE LAB, the key insight is that bleach bypass presets sell because they evoke a specific era of "serious" cinema (late 90s/early 2000s). The look combines desaturation with deep blacks — different from the lifted-shadow A24 aesthetic.

### Confidence
**High** — Technical discussions from cinematographers and lab technicians; process verified across multiple sources.

## 8. Silver Retention Look: Technical Approach and Visual Result

### Claim
Silver retention (ENR process at Technicolor Rome, CCE at Deluxe, ACE at other labs) is a print-only photochemical technique that adds controlled amounts of metallic silver back into the print, creating deeper blacks than standard D-Max, increased shadow contrast, and desaturated colors. Digital emulation uses contrast enhancement, black level crushing, and grain addition.

### Source
Cinematography.net / Roger Deakins Forum / Scribd / LiftGammaGain

### URLs
- https://www.cinematography.net/edited-pages/ENR_Transfer.htm [^488^]
- https://www.rogerdeakins.com/forums/topic/enr-process-with-digital/ [^484^]
- https://www.scribd.com/document/49881103/ENR [^489^]
- https://liftgammagain.com/forum/index.php?threads/how-does-pre-flashing-negative-film-alter-the-look.19432/latest [^487^]

### Date
2005-2023

### Excerpts
> "ENR is a print processing technique that uses additional chemical baths to retain a controlled amount of silver in the print, increasing density and contrast compared to standard processing. It can achieve effects similar to bleach bypass but allows for more precise control." [^489^]

> "The result when silver was left in a print was: (1) deeper blacks than the D-Max of the print stock, (2) less shadow detail due to increased contrast in the shadows, (3) darker colors that were somewhat desaturated from the addition of black silver in the dyes, (4) increase graininess in the print." [^484^]

> "You can emulate the ENR or bleach-bypass process on telecine to a certain extent, but not entirely, as the silver retention in the print leads to deeper maximum blacks, whereas the black level on a TV monitor cannot be exceeded." [^488^]

> "Flashing the negative increases the base fog level, lifting the blacks and bringing up some shadow detail... Leaving a little amount of black silver in a print will deepen the blacks and darken the colors, slightly softening them — it's like mixing a tiny amount of black paint into colored paints." [^487^]

### Context
ENR (named after Technicolor Rome sensitometrist Enrico Novelli Rimo) is the predecessor to modern "crushed black" aesthetics. Spielberg/Kaminski used it extensively. For preset design, the key parameters are: deep blacks (0-5 IRE), lifted shadows relative to the crushed point, desaturated mids, and added texture/grain. The inability to fully replicate silver retention digitally (due to display black level limitations) creates an ongoing market for increasingly sophisticated emulation techniques.

### Confidence
**High** — Technical documentation from cinematography professionals and lab technicians.

## 9. Day-for-Night Color Grading Techniques

### Claim
Day-for-night is achieved by reducing luminance and contrast, cooling color balance toward blue/cyan with magenta undertones, desaturating the image, and selectively masking bright areas (sky, clouds) that would betray the day shoot. The technique is experiencing resurgence due to indie filmmaking budget constraints and TikTok/YouTube creator demand for "moonlit" aesthetics.

### Source
Noam Kroll / Clint Till Blog

### URLs
- https://noamkroll.com/color-grading-tutorial-creating-a-day-for-night-look/ [^495^]
- https://clinttill.net/blog/2014/6/10/how-to-color-grade-day-for-night-in-after-effects [^497^]

### Date
2014-2020

### Excerpts
> "The idea is simple: Shoot all of your footage during the day and simply color grade it to look like it was shot at night... achieving a realistic Day For Night look is as much about production as it is about post." [^495^]

> "Step 1 – Neutralize the image. Step 2 – Reduce luminance and contrast. Step 3 – Cool down the color balance. Step 4 – Re-adjusting levels. Step 5 – Custom masks. Step 6 – Final tweaks." [^495^]

> "I pulled the Shadows and Midtones more into the blue areas of the color wheel. You don't want it to look too blue. Always keep the blacks black." [^497^]

### Context
Day-for-night presets serve two markets: (1) indie filmmakers needing budget solutions, and (2) social media creators wanting "nighttime mood" aesthetics without shooting at night. The TikTok/Instagram "night mood" aesthetic (dark blue shadows, desaturated everything, selective warm practical lights) is essentially day-for-night grading applied to portraits and lifestyle content.

### Confidence
**Medium-High** — Established cinematography technique; social media application is inferred from trend analysis.

## 10. Cross-Processing Aesthetics and Modern Digital Equivalents

### Claim
Cross-processing (developing slide film E6 in C-41 negative chemicals, or vice versa) creates high contrast, wild color casts (greens/magentas/yellows), and unpredictable results. It was widely used in 1990s fashion/music photography. Digital equivalents use channel curve manipulation in RGB, split toning (highlights 50, shadows 170), and saturation adjustments.

## 11. HDR vs SDR Color Grading and Preset Design

### Claim
HDR grading (Dolby Vision, HDR10) requires entirely different approaches from SDR. Netflix mandates Dolby Vision 4.0 for Originals with 1000+ nits, P3-D65 color space, and PQ EOTF. Presets designed for SDR (Rec.709, 100 nits, gamma 2.4) will not translate correctly to HDR. The creator economy is largely SDR-native, but HDR-compatible presets are becoming a premium category.

### Source
Netflix Studios / Plasma.film / ColorPlanet Studios / ViewSonic

### URLs
- https://partnerhelp.netflixstudios.com/hc/en-us/articles/360000599948 [^481^]
- https://shop.plasma.film/first-color-grading-in-hdr/ [^483^]

- https://www.viewsonic.com/library/photography/what-is-hdr-hdr-vs-sdr/ [^499^]

### Date
2023-2026

### Excerpts
> "For Netflix, the minimum color space required for HDR content is Rec. 709 or DCI-P3, with a recommended color space of Rec. 2020. Netflix also recommends using a bit depth of at least 10 bits." [^483^]

> "HDR images can display much brighter highlights and darker shadows than SDR images... For HDR10, minimum peak brightness required is 1,000 nits. For Dolby Vision, peak brightness can range from 1,000 nits up to 10,000 nits." [^483^]

> "HDR Grading often requires more scientifically advanced monitors, longer processing times, and experienced human beings. It can be more labour-intensive and expensive than SDR." [^500^]

> "HDR offers more options for expression, including brilliance in shadows, highlights, and dimensional midtones." [^500^]

### Context
For CINEGRADE LAB: The preset market is currently 99% SDR. However, as HDR displays proliferate (phones, laptops, TVs), demand for HDR-aware presets will grow. The technical challenge is that an SDR preset "crushing blacks" may look completely different in HDR where black levels are absolute. HDR presets need to specify luminance ranges and color space — making them inherently more complex (and premium-priced) than SDR presets.

### Confidence
**High** — Technical specifications from streaming platforms and professional color grading facilities.
