## 4. CONFLICT ZONES
*Contradictions between dimensions requiring resolution*

### CZ-01: Subscription vs One-Time Purchase — Which Model Wins?
- **Dim01:** Most premium presets use one-time purchase (Mastin Labs $99, RNI $192, Noble $149)
- **Dim08:** "Subscriptions win when value is continuous... One-time purchases win when the value is finite"
- **Dim08:** "Consumer subscription fatigue is a significant factor"
- **Dim08:** Adobe CC has 38M+ subscribers; VSCO brought back desktop presets as subscription-exclusive ($5/month)
- **Dim01:** Archipelago Quest uses subscription ($36/3 months)
- **Conflict:** Are consumers tired of subscriptions or embracing them?
- **Resolution:** The conflict is segment-dependent. **Professionals** (wedding photographers, colorists) prefer one-time purchases for tools they own. **Hobbyists/casual creators** accept subscriptions for access to large libraries (Envato Elements, Motion Array). The optimal model for CINEGRADE LAB is likely a **hybrid**: one-time premium packs for professionals + optional subscription for evolving library access.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM — Resolution requires market testing

### CZ-02: Does Film Emulation Actually "Look Like Film" or Is It Comfort Food?
- **Dim04:** Steve Yedlin's proprietary algorithms successfully matched film on Knives Out (2019); "audiences subconsciously register authentic vs. fake"
- **Dim02:** Veteran colorist on LiftGammaGain: "The films I've graded that look like film, were shot by DPs who know film well, and the square root of nothin to do with any of the emulation options"; "no one noticed that the film emulation was gone... zero, nada, nothing"
- **Dim04:** "Digital cameras now emulate film aesthetics perfectly (Fujifilm film simulations, VSCO presets)"
- **Conflict:** Is accurate film emulation technically possible and valuable, or is the "film look" about cinematography knowledge rather than post-processing?
- **Resolution:** Both positions are true for different contexts. **For matching film-digital hybrid productions** (The Last Jedi, Knives Out), accurate emulation is essential. **For standalone digital footage**, the "film look" is primarily about evoking a mood/feeling rather than scientific accuracy. The market has room for both: **Pro tier** (scientific accuracy for matching) and **Creative tier** (mood/feel for standalone). The Dim02 colorist's experience reflects that audiences care about the overall image, not the technical pipeline.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM — Resolution requires market segmentation

### CZ-03: Is the Premium Preset Market Growing or Shrinking?
- **Dim01:** "AI adaptive editing is replacing static presets among high-volume professionals (68% adoption rate)"
- **Dim08:** Cinematic LUTs market growing at 10.4% CAGR; photo editing software at 9.3% CAGR
- **Dim04:** Film photography experiencing renaissance; medium format film camera market growing
- **Dim10:** Preset demand driven by TikTok/Instagram viral trends
- **Conflict:** Is AI killing the preset market or is the overall market growing?
- **Resolution:** The market is **segmenting, not shrinking**. The **high-volume professional** segment (wedding photographers processing 1000+ images) is shifting to AI (Imagen AI, Aftershoot). The **creator/enthusiast** segment (social media, indie filmmaking, hobbyist photography) is growing and still wants presets/LUTs for their simplicity, portability, and aesthetic inspiration. The **premium professional** segment (colorists, commercial photographers) wants more sophisticated tools (plugins, AI-assisted workflows). Total market is growing but composition is shifting.
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Multiple dimensions support segmentation thesis

### CZ-04: Teal/Orange — Timeless Convention or Overused Cliche?
- **Dim05/Dim06:** "Teal and orange are near-complementary colors... the dominant cinematic color grading aesthetic"
- **Dim06:** "The teal-orange trend became so overused it was labeled a 'color grading virus'"; backlash since 2013
- **Dim10:** "Modern variations are more subtle"
- **Dim07:** Sam Kolder orange & teal "remains one of the most sought-after aesthetics"
- **Conflict:** Is teal/orange still viable or is it exhausted?
- **Resolution:** Teal/orange is **not dead but evolved**. Hollywood has moved to subtler variations (A24's "not aggressive teal-and-orange"). Social media creators still want recognizable "cinematic" looks. The opportunity is **modernized teal/orange**: less aggressive, more skin-tone-protective, with film grain and lifted shadows (the "A24-inflected" teal/orange). Peter McKinnon's "That Orange & Teal Though!" LUT suggests the naming itself is now self-aware/ironic.
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Multiple dimensions support evolution thesis

### CZ-05: Is ACES Essential or Overkill for Photography Workflows?
- **Dim02:** ACES workflows use LMTs; DaVinci Resolve has built-in LMT 2383 Kodak Print Emulation
- **Dim05:** ACES is "specifically designed for motion picture, not still photography"; "Neither [ACES AP0/AP1] is well supported for photography"
- **Dim09:** Colourlab.ai is "world's first ACES-compatible neural engine"
- **Conflict:** Should CINEGRADE LAB invest in ACES compatibility or focus on standard RGB workflows?
- **Resolution:** **Dual-path approach**: ACES compatibility for the video/cinema segment (essential for Netflix/Hollywood workflows) + standard sRGB/Rec.709/P3 for photography workflows. The still photography market does not need ACES, but the video/filmmaking segment increasingly does. A product that bridges both has competitive advantage.
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Technical specifications clearly define use cases
